# Operations Strategy
**Module:** A | **Priority:** MEDIUM | **Key Question:** What is the role of operations in the business?

---

## 📋 Overview

Operations strategy defines how your company transforms inputs into outputs to create competitive advantage. Think of it like choosing your restaurant's personality — will you be McDonald's (fast and cheap) or a fancy steakhouse (slow and expensive)? The framework has three levels: Mission (your purpose), Operations Objectives using CQDF (Cost, Quality, Delivery, Flexibility — ranked in order of importance), and Management Levers (the tools you use to achieve your goals). You can't be world-class at everything, so you must choose what matters most and align all your operations decisions around those priorities.

**Case:** McDonald's vs. Burger King

**Prerequisites:** None — this is the foundation lecture

## 📖 Core Concepts

### Operations Strategy Framework
**Concepts:** C11 (Operations Strategy / CQDF), C12 (Management Levers)

> A **strategy** is a well-coordinated set of objectives, policies, and plans aimed at securing a **long-term competitive advantage**.

Operations Strategy has **three levels**:

#### 1. Mission
- States a **purpose** for operations and priority among objectives
- Specifies the **primary task** which must be achieved for operations to succeed

#### 2. Operations Objectives (CQDF)
- **Cost** — Low cost, competitive cost, or premium pricing strategy
- **Quality** — Performance, conformance, reliability, durability, etc.
- **Delivery** — Speed and reliability of fulfillment
- **Flexibility** — Volume changes, new products, product mix
- Must be **defined carefully**, **measurable**, and **rank ordered**

#### 3. Management Levers
The tactical tools you use to achieve objectives:
- Facilities Location and Focus
- Vertical Integration
- Supply Chain Management
- Cost Management
- Quality Management
- New Products / New Services
- Inventory Management
- Production Planning & Scheduling
- Capacity Management
- Process & Technology
- HR and People Management

> 💡 **Key Insight:** The mission sets the general direction for operations, the objectives provide measurable targets or goals, and the management levers are the tools you use to achieve the goals.

### CQDF Framework — Operations Objectives
**Concepts:** C11 (Operations Strategy / CQDF)

#### Cost
- Low cost, competitive cost, or premium pricing strategy
- Measured by: $/unit, cost per passenger, cost relative to competitors

#### Quality — Physical Goods
- **Performance** — primary product characteristics
- **Conformance** — meeting specifications
- **Reliability** — frequency of field failures
- **Durability** — length of product lifetime
- **Features** — "bells and whistles"
- **Serviceability** — speed, courtesy of repair
- **Aesthetics** — look, feel, taste
- **Perceived Quality** — brand, reputation

#### Quality — Services (SERVQUAL)
- **Tangibles** — physical facilities, equipment, appearance
- **Reliability** — dependable and accurate service
- **Responsiveness** — willingness to help promptly
- **Assurance** — knowledge and trust
- **Empathy** — caring, individualized attention

#### Delivery
- **Speed** — rapid delivery
- **Reliability** — on-time delivery
- Measured by: on-time %, delivery lead time, # late deliveries

#### Flexibility
- **Volume** — handling seasonal fluctuations
- **New Product/Service Introduction** — speed and frequency
- **Product/Service Mix** — range of offerings

#### Rules for Using CQDF
1. **Define** each objective carefully for your specific context
2. Set **measurable targets** for each
3. **Rank order** them — you cannot excel at all four simultaneously

> 💡 **Key Insight:** All objectives must be defined carefully, have measurable targets, and be rank ordered. You cannot excel at all four simultaneously.

### Winners vs. Qualifiers & the 3 C's
**Concepts:** C11 (Operations Strategy / CQDF)

#### Order Winners vs. Order Qualifiers

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Qualifier** | Must have to *enter* the market | FDA approval for a drug |
| **Winner** | Feature that leads customer to *choose* your product | Fastest delivery time |

#### The 3 C's for Ranking Objectives

When deciding how to rank your CQDF priorities, consider:

1. **Customers** — What do they actually want?
2. **Competitors** — What are they doing well?
3. **Capabilities** — What can you do better than others?

> 💡 **Key Insight:** Focus your operations on order winners, not just order qualifiers. Understand what customers actually value, not what you *think* they should value.

### McDonald's vs. Burger King Analysis
**Concepts:** C11 (Operations Strategy / CQDF)

#### CQDF Comparison Table

| Objective | McDonald's (Traditional) | Burger King (Traditional) |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Cost** | **#1** — Standardized processes, economies of scale, lowest cost/burger | **#4** — Customization increases costs |
| **Quality** | **#2** — Conformance quality: every Big Mac identical | **#1** — Taste quality: flame-grilled superiority |
| **Delivery** | **#3** — Fast service through assembly-line process | **#3** — Made-to-order takes longer |
| **Flexibility** | **#4** — Limited menu, rigid processes | **#2** — "Have it your way" customization |

#### McDonald's "Made for You" Shift
- Required **completely changing** operations objectives ranking
- Had to **redesign management levers** to support new priorities
- Demonstrates that shifting CQDF rankings is a **fundamental operational transformation**, not a minor tweak

> 💡 **Key Insight:** Different CQDF rankings require fundamentally different operational approaches. McDonald's had to redesign their entire system when they shifted from assembly-line to made-to-order.

### Southwest Airlines Case Study
**Concepts:** C11 (Operations Strategy / CQDF), C12 (Management Levers)

#### Interlocking Operations Strategy

Southwest succeeds because **every management lever reinforces their CQDF priorities**:

| Management Lever | How It Supports Strategy |
|-----------------|-------------------------|
| Point-to-point service | Simplifies scheduling, reduces delays |
| Fast turn time (25 min vs. 60 min industry) | Increases aircraft utilization |
| All Boeing 737 aircraft | Reduces maintenance costs, training costs |
| Smaller airports | Lower gate fees, less congestion |
| Simplified boarding (no assigned seats) | Faster turnaround |
| No meals | Reduces cost and turnaround time |
| Southwest culture | Employee satisfaction → customer satisfaction |

#### CQDF Ranking

| Rank | Objective | Definition |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **#1** | Quality | Reliable, safe service |
| **#2** | Delivery | On-time performance, frequent flights |
| **#3** | Cost | Low prices enabled by operational efficiency |
| **#4** | Flexibility | Limited routes, one aircraft type |

> 💡 **Key Insight:** The best operations strategies are supported through many individual elements (levers/tactics) that are consistent with the objectives. Competitors cannot achieve parity by simply copying individual elements — the whole is more than the sum of the parts.

## 📝 Practice Problems

### Problem 1: Southwest Airlines Quick-Turn Analysis
**Source:** Southwest After Class Handouts Haas 2026 | **Concepts:** C11, C12

**Given:**
- Air Time = $115$ minutes (same for SW and Industry Average)
- Turn Time = $25$ minutes (SW) vs. $60$ minutes (Industry Average)
- Southwest has $539$ Boeing 737s
- Each plane costs $\$60M$
- Cost of capital = $10\%$

**Calculate:**
1. Total time for one flight cycle (each airline)
2. Percent increase in time for competitors vs. Southwest
3. Number of planes competitors need to match Southwest's schedule
4. Annual cost difference at $10\%$ cost of capital

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

#### Step 1: Total Flight Cycle Time
$$\text{SW Total} = 115 + 25 = 140 \text{ minutes}$$
$$\text{Industry Total} = 115 + 60 = 175 \text{ minutes}$$

#### Step 2: Percent Increase
$$\text{\% Increase} = \frac{175}{140} = 1.25 = 25\% \text{ more time}$$

#### Step 3: Planes Needed by Competitors
Competitors need $25\%$ more planes to match Southwest's flight frequency:
$$\text{Competitor planes} = 539 \times 1.25 = 674 \text{ planes}$$

#### Step 4: Annual Cost Difference
$$\text{SW fleet cost} = 539 \times \$60M = \$32.34B$$
$$\text{Competitor fleet cost} = 674 \times \$60M = \$40.44B$$
$$\text{Difference} = \$40.44B - \$32.34B = \$8.1B$$
$$\text{Annual cost difference} = \$8.1B \times 10\% = \$810M \text{ per year}$$

> 💡 A **25-minute** turn time advantage translates to **$810M/year** in savings — this is the power of aligned management levers.

</details>

### Problem 2: CQDF Analysis — McDonald's vs. Burger King
**Source:** Operations Strategy After Class Handouts Haas 2026 | **Concepts:** C11

Complete a CQDF analysis for both companies' **traditional** systems. For each company:
1. **Rank** the four objectives ($1$–$4$)
2. **Define** what each objective means for that company
3. Explain how their operational choices support the ranking

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

#### McDonald's Traditional CQDF

| Rank | Objective | Definition | Supporting Levers |
|------|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **#1** | Cost | Lowest cost per burger | Standardized processes, economies of scale, limited menu |
| **#2** | Quality | Conformance quality — every Big Mac identical worldwide | Rigid procedures, training, quality control |
| **#3** | Delivery | Fast service under 60 seconds | Assembly-line process, pre-made inventory |
| **#4** | Flexibility | Minimal menu variations | Rigid process limits customization |

#### Burger King Traditional CQDF

| Rank | Objective | Definition | Supporting Levers |
|------|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **#1** | Quality | Taste quality — flame-grilled superiority | Flame broiler technology, fresh preparation |
| **#2** | Flexibility | Customization — "Have it your way" | Made-to-order process, flexible assembly |
| **#3** | Delivery | Reasonable speed, but made-to-order is slower | Parallel preparation where possible |
| **#4** | Cost | Higher costs due to customization | Customization inherently more expensive |

> 💡 **Key takeaway:** Same industry, completely different strategies. Neither is "wrong" — they serve different customer priorities.

</details>
