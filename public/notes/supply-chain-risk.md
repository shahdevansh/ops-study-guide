# Supply Chain Risk Management

**Module:** C | **Priority:** MEDIUM | **Key Question:** How do we prepare for and respond to supply chain disruptions and geopolitical risks?

---

## 📋 Overview

Supply chain risk management is like **buying insurance before you need it**. Through **anticipatory strategies**, you "buy options" during calm times — backup suppliers, safety stock, flexible capacity. When disruption strikes, you "exercise your options" with **reactive adaptations**. From COVID to geopolitical tensions, modern supply chains face increasing uncertainty that requires systematic **risk assessment** and preparation.

### 2. Risk Management Framework (C46)

**Concepts:** C46

**Definition:** A process designed to **identify potential events** that may affect the entity, and **manage risk** to be within its **risk appetite**.

#### The Risk Equation

$$\text{Risk} = \text{Probability of Event} \times \text{Impact of Event}$$

#### ERM Components

1. **Internal Environment** — organizational tone and risk appetite
2. **Objective Setting** — what are we trying to protect?
3. **Event Identification** — what could go wrong? (internal + external)
4. **Risk Assessment** — likelihood × impact matrix
5. **Risk Response** — **Avoid**, **Accept**, **Reduce**, or **Share** risk
6. **Control Activities** — policies and procedures
7. **Monitoring** — ongoing surveillance

> **Risk appetite** is the key strategic decision: how much risk are you willing to tolerate? A pharmaceutical company has very low risk appetite for quality failures; a tech startup might accept high risk for speed to market.

### 3. Risk Assessment Matrix (C46)

**Concepts:** C46

### Categorizing Risks by Likelihood × Impact

#### The Matrix

|  | **Negligible** | **Low** | **Moderate** | **Very High** | **Extreme** |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **Almost Certain** | Medium | High | High | Very High | Very High |
| **Likely** | Low | Medium | High | High | Very High |
| **Moderate** | Low | Medium | Medium | High | High |
| **Unlikely** | Low | Low | Medium | Medium | High |
| **Rare** | Low | Low | Low | Medium | Medium |

#### Common Supply Chain Risks (High Probability / Moderate Impact)

- Matching supply with demand
- Delayed shipments
- Demand fluctuations
- Financial risk / currency exchange rates
- Moderate tariff changes

#### Black Swan Risks (Low Probability / Extreme Impact)

- **Natural disasters** — volcano, tsunami, earthquake
- **Geopolitical** — war, political instability, large tariff changes
- **Health** — pandemic (COVID, avian flu)
- **Industrial** — supplier factory fire, oil spill
- **Financial** — market crash

> **Different risks require different strategies:** Common risks → optimize and hedge. Black swans → build resilience and options.

### 4. Two-Stage Risk Management Strategy (C46, C47)

**Concepts:** C46, C47

### "Buy Options" → "Exercise Options"

| Stage | When | What You Do | Analogy |
|-------|------|-------------|--------|
| **Anticipatory** | Before disruption | Build capabilities, create options | Buying insurance |
| **Reactive** | During disruption | Use pre-built capabilities | Filing a claim |

#### Anticipatory Strategies ("Buy Options")

**1. Information & Planning**
- Scenario planning for various disruption types
- Real-time risk monitoring systems
- Supply network modeling and optimization

**2. Supply Chain Structure Design**
- **Footprint restructuring** & multi-region sourcing
- **China+1 / China+Many** diversification
- Strategic sourcing partnerships
- **Vertical integration** of critical suppliers

**3. Supply Chain Process Adjustments**
- Strategic inventory buffers (safety stock)
- Directed buy agreements
- Process streamlining (blockchain for reduced paperwork)

> **Key insight:** Anticipatory strategies are **investments** made during calm times. They cost money upfront but create valuable **options** you can exercise when crisis hits.

### 5. Supply Chain Structure Redesign (C47)

**Concepts:** C47

### Geographic Diversification Strategies

#### China+1 / China+Many

| Strategy | Description | Tradeoff |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Single source (China)** | 100% from one country | Lowest cost, highest risk |
| **China+1** | Primary in China, backup in Vietnam/India/Mexico | Moderate cost increase, significant risk reduction |
| **China+Many** | Spread across 3-4 countries | Higher cost, lowest risk |
| **Full diversification** | No country >30% | Highest cost, maximum resilience |

#### Three Structure Strategies

1. **Footprint restructuring & multi-region sourcing**
   - Multiple suppliers in different regions
   - Closer to end markets
   - Exchange rate risk mitigation
   - Optimize production-distribution networks

2. **Strategic sourcing partnerships**
   - Jointly pursue resilience strategies with key suppliers
   - Shared risk monitoring
   - Long-term commitments in exchange for priority allocation

3. **Vertical integration of critical suppliers**
   - Control location and priority allocation
   - Eliminate dependency on external decisions
   - Higher cost but maximum control

> **The tradeoff is always cost vs. resilience.** A perfectly diversified supply chain is expensive; a perfectly efficient one is fragile.

### 6. Buffers and Flexibility (C48)

**Concepts:** C48

### Managing Common Supply Chain Risks

#### Types of Buffers

| Buffer Type | What It Is | Cost | Best For |
|-------------|-----------|------|----------|
| **Inventory buffer** (Safety stock) | Extra inventory above expected demand | Holding costs | Demand variability, supply delays |
| **Time buffer** (Safety time) | Ordering earlier than needed | Working capital tied up longer | Lead time uncertainty |
| **Information buffer** | Shared data with partners | IT investment | Demand signal distortion |
| **Capacity buffer** | Extra production capability | Idle resources | Demand spikes, supply disruption |

#### Safety Stock Formula

$$\text{Safety Stock} = f(\text{avg lead time demand}, \text{variability}, \text{service level})$$

- **Higher variability** → more safety stock needed
- **Higher service level target** → more safety stock needed
- **Longer lead times** → more safety stock needed

#### Information-Based Strategies

- **S&OP** (Sales & Operations Planning) — internal coordination
- **CPFR** (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting & Replenishment) — external sharing
- **VMI** (Vendor Managed Inventory) — supplier manages your stock

> **Alternative to holding inventory:** Use **flexibility and redundancy** — reactive capacity, currency hedging, multiple transportation modes.

### 7. Reactive Adaptations (C47)

**Concepts:** C47

### "Exercise Your Options" When Disruption Hits

#### What You Do During a Crisis

1. **Shift sourcing or production** dynamically across regions
2. **Invoke priority allocation** from pre-qualified suppliers
3. **Adjust inventory and purchase agreements** — expedite, redirect, substitute
4. **Activate backup suppliers** and contingency capacity
5. **Real-time monitoring** enables rapid detection and response

#### The Key: Pre-Built Capabilities

> You can only exercise options you've already purchased. A company that invested in backup suppliers can switch in days. A company that didn't may need **months** to qualify new suppliers.

#### Cross-Functional Coordination During Crisis

| Function | Actions |
|----------|---------|
| **Operations** | Shift production, activate backups, expedite |
| **Finance** | Hedge currency, assess working capital impact, accelerate payments |
| **HR** | Employee safety, evacuations, cross-training activation |
| **Marketing** | Communicate shortages, manage customer expectations |
| **CEO** | Strategic decisions on long-term sourcing changes |

> **Supply chain risk management requires coordination across ALL functions** — it's not just an operations problem.

### Problem 1: Furniture Company Risk Scenario — Phase 1
**Source:** Supply Chain Risk Management After Class Handouts | **Concepts:** C46, C47

A furniture company manufactures in **China (two factories: Kunming & Guangzhou)** and **North Carolina**.

Three risks emerge simultaneously:
1. **Heavy snows in Siberia** shut highways and rail lines (4-day closure expected)
2. **Tensions between Taiwan and Mainland China** escalating
3. **New Avian influenza outbreak** in southwest China near the Kunming factory

As Operations Manager, **what actions would you take for each risk?**

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

#### Risk 1: Siberia Snow Closure (4 days)
- **Assess:** Monitor container availability at ports; determine which shipments are in transit
- **React:** Consider **air freight** for critical/time-sensitive shipments
- **Communicate:** Notify customers of potential 4-7 day delays
- **Severity:** Low-moderate — temporary disruption with known timeline

#### Risk 2: Taiwan-China Tensions
- **Assess:** Review **currency hedge positions** and exposure
- **React:** Evaluate **alternative shipping routes** (avoid Taiwan Strait if needed)
- **Prepare:** Develop contingency production plans for non-China facilities
- **Severity:** Medium — monitor closely, prepare but don't overreact yet

#### Risk 3: Avian Influenza near Kunming
- **Assess:** Monitor **WHO classifications** for pandemic risk escalation
- **React:** Review employee safety protocols at Kunming factory
- **Prepare:** Plan to **shift production to Guangzhou** factory if Kunming must close
- **Severity:** Medium-high — direct threat to one facility

> **Key principle:** Each risk requires balancing **cost of action vs. risk mitigation**. Don't overreact to low-probability events, but have plans ready for escalation.

### Problem 2: Designing a China+1 Strategy
**Source:** Supply Chain Risk Management After Class Handouts | **Concepts:** C47

A company currently sources **80% of components from China**. Design a **"China+1" strategy** that reduces geopolitical risk while managing costs.

**What are the tradeoffs?**

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

#### Proposed Structure

| Region | Volume Share | Role |
|--------|:---:|------|
| **China** | 60-70% | Primary — maintain scale economics |
| **Vietnam** | 15-20% | Secondary — labor cost advantage |
| **Mexico** | 10-15% | Nearshore — fast delivery to US market |
| **India** | 5-10% | Emerging — long-term diversification |

#### Implementation Timeline: 2-3 Years

1. **Year 1:** Qualify new suppliers, pilot small orders
2. **Year 2:** Ramp to target volumes, build relationships
3. **Year 3:** Full diversification, optimize logistics

#### Tradeoffs

| Benefit | Cost |
|---------|------|
| ✅ Reduced geopolitical risk | ❌ Higher unit costs (new suppliers lack scale) |
| ✅ Reduced tariff exposure | ❌ Management complexity (multiple regions) |
| ✅ Supply continuity if one region disrupted | ❌ Longer qualification time |
| ✅ Potential nearshoring speed advantage | ❌ Quality consistency challenges initially |

> **Critical insight:** Gradual transition over 2-3 years allows supplier development and cost optimization. Don't try to shift overnight — that's a different kind of disruption.

</details>
